Moabite Stone: Earliest Inscription

(c. 840 BCE)

The Moabite Stone, also known as the Mesha Stele, provides the earliest extra-biblical evidence of Jehovah’s name, “YHWH,” confirming its central role in true worship long before later attempts to obscure it, as declared: “This is my name forever” (Exodus 3:15). This ancient artifact stands as a powerful witness to Jehovah’s prominence in Israel’s history.

Moabite Stone (Mesha Stele): Discovered in 1868 at Dhiban, Jordan, this basalt stele was inscribed by King Mesha of Moab around 840 BCE, recounting his successful revolt against Israel, as recorded in Scripture (2 Kings 3:4-27). Line 18 explicitly mentions capturing “vessels of YHWH” from a sanctuary at Nebo, verifying that Jehovah’s name was integral to Israel’s worship practices. Housed in the Louvre Museum, Paris, with a replica in the British Museum, London, the stele’s authenticity is confirmed through radiocarbon dating and paleographic analysis, aligning it with the 9th century BCE. Its testimony, etched by an enemy king, underscores the undeniable prominence of “YHWH” in ancient Israel, fulfilling “My people will know my name” (Isaiah 52:6).

Vindicating Jehovah’s Name

The Moabite Stone’s reference to “YHWH,” carved by a rival ruler, directly counters Satan’s efforts to erase Jehovah’s name from human consciousness, as “the god of this system of things has blinded the minds of the unbelievers” (2 Corinthians 4:4). This artifact proves that Jehovah’s name was not only known but revered in Israel, even acknowledged by its enemies. Silver Scrolls and Lachish Letters

(c. 600–590 BCE)

Just before Babylon’s conquest of Judah, the Silver Scrolls and Lachish Letters preserved Jehovah’s name, “YHWH,” demonstrating its widespread use in daily life and worship, as urged: “Call on the name of Jehovah” (Joel 2:32). These artifacts provide tangible evidence of the name’s significance in Judahite society.

Silver Scrolls (Kuntillet Ajrud): Unearthed in 1975 in southern Israel, these two tiny silver amulets, dated to around 600 BCE, are inscribed with blessings invoking “YHWH,” such as “May you be blessed by YHWH of Samaria and by His Asherah.” While the reference to Asherah reflects pagan influences, the use of “YHWH” confirms that Jehovah’s name was central to personal devotion among the Israelites. Displayed in the Israel Museum, Jerusalem, these scrolls, validated by archaeological analysis, highlight the name’s role in private worship, even in a time of spiritual decline.

Lachish Letters: Discovered in 1935 at the Lachish fortress, these ostraca (pottery shards) from around 590 BCE include military correspondence mentioning “YHWH,” such as “May YHWH cause my lord to hear tidings of peace today.” Written just before Jerusalem’s fall, they show that Jehovah’s name was used in everyday communication among Judah’s leaders. Preserved in the Israel Museum, Jerusalem, these letters affirm the name’s integration into both religious and secular life, resisting efforts to diminish its prominence.

Enduring Evidence

Validated by rigorous archaeological methods, including paleography and contextual analysis, the Silver Scrolls and Lachish Letters prove that “YHWH” was a familiar and cherished name across all levels of Judahite society. Their survival counters later attempts by false religion to replace Jehovah’s name with titles like “Lord,” ensuring that “the word of Jehovah endures forever” (1 Peter 1:25). These artifacts stand as a testament to Jehovah’s promise that His name would remain known, strengthening the faithful to proclaim it boldly in the face of opposition.

Dead Sea Scrolls: Preserving YHWH

Dead Sea Scrolls: Discovered between 1947 and 1956 in the Qumran caves near the Dead Sea, these 900+ manuscripts, dated from 200 BCE to 70 CE, include nearly every book of the Hebrew Scriptures. The Great Isaiah Scroll (1QIsa^a), one of the most complete, prominently uses “YHWH” in paleo-Hebrew script, as seen in Isaiah 42:8: “I am Jehovah, that is my name.” This deliberate use of an archaic script underscores the name’s sanctity, distinguishing it from other terms. Housed in the Israel Museum’s Shrine of the Book, Jerusalem, and the Jordan Museum, Amman, the scrolls, dated through carbon-14 and paleographic analysis, were likely preserved by the Essenes, a Jewish sect dedicated to scriptural purity. Their work ensured that Jehovah’s name remained intact, fulfilling “The word of our God endures forever” (Isaiah 40:8).

Stored in the Cairo Museum, this fragment, dated through paleographic analysis, reflects the reverence of Jewish scholars in Alexandria for Jehovah’s name. These translators, working around 280–150 BCE, ensured that Greek-speaking Jews in the diaspora could honor the divine name, countering the influence of pagan philosophies that sought to diminish its significance.

Early Codices: Sinaiticus and Vaticanus

(c. 350 CE)

These manuscripts played a vital role in safeguarding the Scriptures during a time of spiritual corruption, though they faced challenges that required later correction.

Codex Sinaiticus: Compiled around 350 CE, this is one of the earliest complete Greek Bibles, discovered at Saint Catherine’s Monastery on Mount Sinai and now housed in the British Library, London. It occasionally retains “YHWH” in Hebrew script within Old Testament quotations, particularly in passages like Psalm 83:18, showing that some scribes recognized the name’s importance. However, its inconsistent use of “YHWH” and inclusion of uninspired texts, such as the Apocrypha (e.g., Tobit), reflect the influence of apostate trends that sought to dilute true worship. The codex’s preservation of early Christian Scriptures, dated through paleographic analysis and radiocarbon testing, provided a critical link to the original texts, despite these flaws.

Codex Vaticanus: Also dated to around 350 CE, this Greek manuscript, stored in the Vatican Library, Rome, preserves Old Testament texts where “YHWH” appeared in the Hebrew originals, though it typically uses “Kyrios” due to prevailing Jewish and Christian traditions avoiding the divine name. Like Sinaiticus, it includes uninspired Apocryphal books and lacks certain passages, such as Mark 16:9-20, indicating textual variations. Its survival, validated by archaeological methods, ensured that traces of Jehovah’s name endured, even as false religion sought to obscure it.

Masoretic Texts: Aleppo and Leningrad Codices

(c. 930–1008 CE)

In the Middle Ages, Jewish scribes known as Masoretes meticulously preserved Jehovah’s name, “YHWH,” in the Hebrew Scriptures, countering efforts by false religion to remove it, as Satan, “the dragon, was enraged with the woman” (Revelation 12:17). Their work ensured that Jehovah’s name remained clear and accessible for future generations.

Aleppo Codex (c. 930 CE): Crafted in Tiberias, this Masoretic Hebrew Bible is one of the most authoritative texts of the Hebrew Scriptures, containing “YHWH” 6,973 times, with vowel points indicating the pronunciation “Jehovah.” Housed in the Israel Museum, Jerusalem, the codex, dated through paleographic analysis, reflects the Masoretes’ rigorous methods, such as counting letters and verses to ensure accuracy. Its preservation of “YHWH” in every instance where it appeared in the original texts provided a reliable source for later translations, resisting centuries of attempts to replace the divine name with titles.

Leningrad Codex (c. 1008 CE): The oldest complete Hebrew Bible, this manuscript, located in the National Library of Russia, St. Petersburg, also includes “YHWH” 6,973 times, with precise vowel points for “Jehovah.” Copied in Cairo, it aligns closely with the Aleppo Codex, confirming the Masoretes’ unparalleled accuracy. Its detailed annotations, or Masorah, safeguarded the text against errors, ensuring that Jehovah’s name and His Word remained pure, as “the word of our God endures forever” (Isaiah 40:8). The Leningrad Codex serves as a primary source for modern translations, including the New World Translation.

Masoretic Fidelity

The Masoretes’ commitment to preserving “YHWH” with vowel points clarified its pronunciation as “Jehovah,” directly fulfilling the declaration, “You, whose name is Jehovah, you alone are the Most High” (Psalm 83:18). Unlike earlier codices that sometimes used “Kyrios” or included uninspired texts, the Masoretic texts are remarkably free of significant flaws, offering a pure transmission of the Hebrew Scriptures.
