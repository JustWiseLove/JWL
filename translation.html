Hebrew Scriptures: The Inspired Foundation

(c. 1500–400 BCE)

Jehovah’s inspired Word in Hebrew anchors true worship, as “all Scripture is inspired of God” (2 Timothy 3:16).

Original Manuscripts: Written by Moses and prophets, these texts used “YHWH” 6,973 times, proclaiming “I am Jehovah, that is my name” (Isaiah 42:8). Scribes ensured accuracy, fulfilling “the word of Jehovah endures” (1 Peter 1:25).

Historical Support

The Silver Scrolls (c. 600 BCE, Israel Museum) confirm “YHWH” in daily use. Radiocarbon dating validates their antiquity, proving scribal fidelity.

Septuagint: Greek Expansion

(c. 280–150 BCE)

The Septuagint brought Jehovah’s Word to Greek-speaking Jews, preserving His name, as “My people will know my name” (Isaiah 52:6).

Septuagint (LXX): Translated in Alexandria, Egypt, it retained “YHWH” in Hebrew characters, as seen in Papyrus Fouad 266 (2nd century BCE, Cairo Museum), resisting the use of “Kyrios” (Lord).

Scientific Support

Paleographic analysis dates these fragments to the 2nd century BCE, confirming authenticity. Its spread strengthened worship among diaspora Jews.

“This is my name forever.” — Exodus 3:15

Dead Sea Scrolls: Preserving Purity

(c. 200 BCE–70 CE)

The Dead Sea Scrolls safeguarded Jehovah’s Word against corruption, as “Your word is truth” (John 17:17).

Qumran Manuscripts: Found in 1947–1956, these 900+ scrolls include “YHWH” in paleo-Hebrew script, as in the Great Isaiah Scroll (Israel Museum, Jerusalem). They confirm the Hebrew text’s purity.

Scientific Support

Carbon-14 dating places the scrolls between 200 BCE and 70 CE, verifying their age. Their alignment with Masoretic texts proves scribal precision.

“My words will not pass away.” — Matthew 24:35

Early Codices: Enduring Amid Apostasy

(c. 325–350 CE)

Greek codices preserved Jehovah’s Word as apostasy grew, as “the word of Jehovah endures” (1 Peter 1:25).

Codex Sinaiticus: Found at Mount Sinai, it retains traces of “YHWH” in Old Testament quotes (British Library, London). Codex Vaticanus: Stored in the Vatican, it preserves texts where “YHWH” appeared, though using “Kyrios.”

Scientific Support

Paleographic studies date these to the 4th century, confirming their early origin. Their texts align with the Septuagint, ensuring reliability.

“I am Jehovah, that is my name.” — Isaiah 42:8

Latin Vulgate: Widespread but Flawed

(c. 382–405 CE)

The Vulgate spread Jehovah’s Word in Latin but obscured His name, as “the word of God is alive” yet requires clarity (Hebrews 4:12).

Jerome’s Vulgate: Translated by Jerome in Bethlehem, it replaced “YHWH” with “Dominus” (Lord). Preserved in the Vatican Library, it dominated for centuries.

Historical Support

Manuscripts like Codex Amiatinus (8th century, Florence) show its influence. Its widespread use shaped medieval worship, despite the name’s absence.

Early Modern Translations: Progress and Pitfalls

(1382–1885 CE)

Early modern translations made Jehovah’s Word accessible but introduced errors, as “Your word is truth” demands precision (John 17:17).

Wycliffe Bible (1382): The first English Bible, based on the Vulgate, used “Lord” instead of “YHWH,” obscuring “This is my name forever” (Exodus 3:15). Its handwritten copies (British Library) were banned for challenging church authority.

Tyndale Bible (1526): William Tyndale used “Jehovah” in places (e.g., Exodus 6:3), defying bans. Executed in 1536, his work (British Library) influenced later Bibles but relied on limited manuscripts.

Geneva Bible (1560): Popular among Puritans, it used “LORD” and included Calvinist notes, introducing bias. Its reliance on the Textus Receptus limited accuracy (British Library).

King James Version (1611): Commissioned by King James I, it used “LORD” except in four places (e.g., Psalm 83:18). Based on the Textus Receptus, it includes additions like 1 John 5:7, absent in early manuscripts (e.g., Sinaiticus), and archaic language confuses readers.

Revised Version (1885): Aimed to update the KJV, it retained “LORD” and relied on newer manuscripts but introduced inconsistent renderings, weakening clarity.

Common Faults

1. Removal of Jehovah’s Name: Replacing “YHWH” with “Lord” hides “My people will know my name” (Isaiah 52:6).
2. Spurious Texts: Additions like 1 John 5:7, likely copyist notes, appear in later manuscripts but not in early codices.
3. Limited Manuscripts: Reliance on late sources (e.g., Textus Receptus) ignores older texts like the Dead Sea Scrolls.
4. Doctrinal Bias or Archaic Language: Notes (Geneva) or outdated terms (KJV) obscure “Your word is a lamp” (Psalm 119:105).

Historical Support

Textual criticism, comparing these to codices like Vaticanus (4th century), reveals their flaws. Their influence (e.g., KJV’s global spread) contrasts with their inaccuracies.

Modern Translations: Influence and Inaccuracies

(1901–Present)

Modern translations gained popularity but strayed from Jehovah’s truth, as “all Scripture is inspired” requires fidelity (2 Timothy 3:16).

American Standard Version (1901): Restored “Jehovah” in the Old Testament but used “Lord” in New Testament quotes, diluting “This is my name forever” (Exodus 3:15). It retained questionable texts (e.g., Mark 16:9-20).

Revised Standard Version (1952): Used “LORD” broadly, with “Jehovah” in rare cases. Its liberal renderings (e.g., Isaiah 7:14 as “young woman”) sparked controversy for weakening prophecy.

New American Standard Bible (NASB, 1971): More literal, it still used “LORD” and included spurious texts (e.g., John 7:53–8:11), unsupported by early manuscripts like Sinaiticus.

New International Version (NIV, 1978): Popular but flawed, it omits “Jehovah,” uses dynamic equivalence (paraphrasing), and retains additions (e.g., Mark 16:9-20), reducing fidelity to “Your word is truth” (John 17:17).

New King James Version (NKJV, 1982): Updates the KJV but retains “LORD” and errors like 1 John 5:7, ignoring older manuscripts like the Dead Sea Scrolls.

English Standard Version (ESV, 2001): Literal but uses “LORD” and includes questionable texts, failing to reflect “My people will know my name” (Isaiah 52:6).

Common Faults

1. Removal of Jehovah’s Name: Substituting “LORD” obscures Jehovah’s identity.
2. Spurious Additions: Texts like 1 John 5:7 and John 7:53–8:11, likely marginal notes, lack early manuscript support.
3. Inaccurate Sources: Over-reliance on later manuscripts ignores older, reliable texts.
4. Paraphrasing: NIV’s loose renderings sacrifice precision, unlike “Your word is a lamp” (Psalm 119:105).

Historical Support

Textual criticism reveals their reliance on late manuscripts. The NIV’s 450 million copies show influence, but comparison to codices like Vaticanus highlights inaccuracies.

Over 240 million copies in 200+ languages ensure global reach.

For example, Mark 16:9-20 appears in later manuscripts (5th century) but not in Sinaiticus, ensuring “Your word is truth” (John 17:17).

4. Clear, Modern Language: Precise English clarifies meaning for all, as “Your word is a lamp” (Psalm 119:105).
5. Scholarly Rigor: Cross-references thousands of manuscripts, avoiding doctrinal bias, unlike translations swayed by tradition (e.g., NIV’s paraphrasing).
